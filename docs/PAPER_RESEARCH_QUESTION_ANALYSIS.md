## Current Situation: What Do We Actually Have?

### Our Model Results Show:

1. **Technology comparison**: NCC-H₂ vs NCC-Electricity cost difference is 5-10%
2. **Production pathway dominates**: Restructuring saves $18B (58% cost reduction)
3. **Hydrogen infrastructure**: 7.7 Mt/year requires 15 GW electrolyzer capacity
4. **Both pathways viable**: Cost difference is small compared to total investment

### The Problem: What's NOT Clear

Looking at our results, several potential research questions emerge, but which is most important?

---

## Potential Research Questions (Let's Debate Each)

### Option A: "Which Technology Pathway is Better?"
**Hypothesis**: NCC-H₂ is more cost-effective than NCC-Electricity for petrochemical decarbonization

**Pros**:
- Clear binary comparison
- Industry needs this answer
- Literature has conflicting views

**Cons**:
- **OUR RESULT**: Only 5-10% difference - is this significant?
- Answer may be "it doesn't matter much" (weak conclusion)
- Ignores the bigger finding about production pathways

**Academic Value**: ⭐⭐ (Incremental, not groundbreaking)

---

### Option B: "Production Strategy vs Technology Choice"
**Hypothesis**: Production pathway restructuring has greater impact on decarbonization cost than technology choice

**Our Evidence**:
- Technology choice: $1.5-1.9B difference (5-10%)
- Production pathway: $18B difference (58%)
- **Production pathway is 10x more important than technology choice!**

**Pros**:
- Counter-intuitive finding (most papers focus on technology)
- High policy relevance (challenges conventional wisdom)
- Our data strongly supports this

**Cons**:
- Requires justifying why production restructuring is feasible
- May be controversial (industry won't like hearing "shrink production")

**Academic Value**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Novel insight, challenges conventional focus)

---

### Option C: "Hydrogen Infrastructure Feasibility"
**Hypothesis**: Green hydrogen pathway is infrastructure-limited, not cost-limited

**Our Evidence**:
- H₂ cost competitive (only 5-10% premium for alternative)
- BUT requires 15 GW electrolyzer (massive infrastructure)
- Rapid deployment needed 2030-2040 (>2 GW/year)

**Pros**:
- Practical bottleneck identification
- Links technology economics to infrastructure reality
- Policy-relevant

**Cons**:
- Hydrogen infrastructure is well-studied topic
- Less novel theoretically

**Academic Value**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Important but incremental)

---

### Option D: "Technology-Neutral Decarbonization Pathways"
**Hypothesis**: Multiple technology pathways can achieve similar emissions outcomes at comparable costs

**Our Evidence**:
- H₂ and Electricity achieve SAME emissions (by design)
- Cost difference only 5-10%
- Implies policy should be technology-neutral

**Pros**:
- Policy-relevant (don't pick winners)
- Supported by robust modeling
- Aligns with economic theory

**Cons**:
- May seem obvious to economists
- Less exciting narrative

**Academic Value**: ⭐⭐⭐ (Solid policy message)

---

### Option E: "The Industry Restructuring Dilemma" (RECOMMENDED)
**Research Question**:
> "What is the optimal balance between technology deployment and industrial restructuring for deep decarbonization of hard-to-abate sectors?"

**Core Hypothesis**:
> "For carbon-intensive, commodity industries, production restructuring (capacity reduction) offers greater cost-effectiveness than technology-only pathways, but faces higher political and economic barriers"

**Sub-hypotheses**:
1. Production restructuring reduces decarbonization costs by >50% compared to growth scenarios
2. Technology choice (H₂ vs Electricity) has minimal impact (<10%) on total cost
3. Optimal strategy combines moderate restructuring (25-40%) with technology deployment

**Why This is Novel**:
- Most MACC studies assume production growth or stability
- Few quantify the cost-benefit of demand reduction vs technology
- Addresses the "elephant in the room" for hard-to-abate sectors
- Highly relevant for Net Zero 2050 pathways

**Our Unique Contribution**:
- First facility-level MACC analysis comparing production pathways
- Quantifies the "restructuring dividend" ($18B savings)
- Shows technology debates are secondary to production strategy
- Case study: South Korea petrochemicals (globally significant)

---

## Why Option E is Strongest

### 1. **Originality**
- Literature focuses on: "How to decarbonize existing production"
- We ask: "Should we decarbonize existing production OR restructure?"
- This is the uncomfortable question policymakers avoid

### 2. **Policy Relevance**
- Net Zero requires demand reduction in hard-to-abate sectors
- Quantifies the economic incentive for restructuring
- Challenges growth-based industrial policy

### 3. **Theoretical Contribution**
- Extends MACC methodology to include production scenarios
- Shows opportunity cost of technology-only approaches
- Bridges technology assessment and industrial policy

### 4. **Practical Impact**
- Korea's petrochemical overcapacity is real policy issue
- Applicable to other regions (EU, Japan facing similar issues)
- Provides decision framework for policymakers

---

## Refined Research Question

### Primary Research Question:
**"How do production restructuring strategies compare to technology pathways for achieving cost-effective petrochemical decarbonization?"**

### Specific Hypotheses to Test:

**H1 (Production Dominance)**:
Production pathway choice has 5-10x greater impact on decarbonization cost than technology pathway choice

**H2 (Technology Neutrality)**:
Hydrogen-fueled and electrically-heated naphtha crackers achieve comparable emissions reductions at similar costs (<10% differential)

**H3 (Optimal Restructuring)**:
Moderate capacity reduction (25-40%) minimizes total decarbonization cost while maintaining industrial viability

**H4 (Infrastructure Bottleneck)**:
Hydrogen pathway faces infrastructure deployment constraints (15 GW electrolyzer in 15 years) despite cost competitiveness

This suggests that industrial policy debates should prioritize production strategy over technology selection."

## Questions to Answer Before Proceeding

### Critical Questions:

1. **Feasibility**: Is 25-40% production reduction realistic for Korea?
   - Need context: overcapacity, export markets, domestic demand
   - Can we justify this isn't just "shut down the industry"?

2. **Generalizability**: Does this apply beyond Korea?
   - EU also has petrochemical overcapacity
   - Japan, US facing similar issues
   - Can we claim broader lessons?

3. **Political Sensitivity**: Will this be seen as "anti-industry"?
   - Frame as "efficiency" not "degrowth"
   - Emphasize "optimal restructuring" not "shut down"
   - Link to circular economy, material efficiency

4. **Data Limitations**: What assumptions are most critical?
   - Facility retirement (50-year lifetime)
   - Technology costs (literature-validated)
   - H₂ and RE prices (conservative assumptions)
