# Literature Review

## 2.1. Decarbonization of the Petrochemical Industry

The global petrochemical sector accounts for approximately 14% of industrial energy use and 5% of global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions (IEA, 2024). As countries commit to net-zero targets, the industry faces an existential challenge: decoupling production growth from carbon emissions while maintaining global competitiveness (Davis et al., 2023). The energy-intensive steam cracking process, which produces olefins like ethylene and propylene, represents the primary source of emissions, requiring temperatures of roughly 850°C traditionally achieved through fossil fuel combustion (Bressan, 2023).

### 2.1.1. Electrification of Steam Crackers

Electrification of cracking furnaces ("e-cracking") has emerged as a promising deep decarbonization pathway. Recent technological assessments demonstrate that electric resistance heating can eliminate direct combustion emissions, reducing CO2 emissions by 55-95% depending on grid carbon intensity (Linde Engineering, 2023; Fernandez et al., 2022). European industry initiatives like the "Cracker of the Future" consortium have demonstrated pilot-scale e-cracking with 30% energy efficiency improvements compared to conventional furnaces (European Chemical Industry Council, 2023).

However, e-cracking presents significant infrastructure challenges. A single large-scale ethylene cracker (1 MTA capacity) requires approximately 500-700 MW of electricity demand—equivalent to a small city's consumption (Bressan, 2023). For industrial clusters like South Korea's Ulsan complex, with multiple crackers, this translates to gigawatt-scale grid reinforcement requirements (Ren & Pauliuk, 2023).

### 2.1.2. Hydrogen Utilization Pathways

Hydrogen presents a dual decarbonization opportunity: as a clean combustion fuel for high-temperature heat and as a feedstock for chemical processes (IEA, 2024). Recent techno-economic analyses suggest that hydrogen-fired crackers can achieve 40-60% emission reductions compared to natural gas, contingent on hydrogen production pathways (Vakkilainen et al., 2023).

The economic viability of hydrogen pathways depends heavily on regional factors. Studies indicate that green hydrogen costs must fall below $2/kg to compete with natural gas in petrochemical applications, requiring significant renewable energy expansion and electrolyzer cost reductions (IEA, 2024; Borello et al., 2023).

### 2.1.3. Emerging Technologies and Process Integration

Beyond electrification and hydrogen, researchers are exploring carbon capture and utilization (CCU), bio-based feedstocks, and novel catalytic processes (Davis et al., 2023). Recent assessments suggest that CCU integration with existing crackers could capture 80-90% of CO2 emissions at $50-100/tCO2, though storage capacity and utilization markets remain challenges (Bui et al., 2023).

## 2.2. Marginal Abatement Cost Analysis in Industrial Decarbonization

The Marginal Abatement Cost (MAC) curve methodology has become a cornerstone analytical framework for climate mitigation planning, particularly in industrial sectors where technology pathways are diverse and capital-intensive (Wang et al., 2023). MAC analysis provides policymakers with a systematic approach to prioritize investments based on cost-effectiveness, identifying "low-hanging fruit" versus expensive deep decarbonization measures (Morris et al., 2022).

### 2.2.1. MAC Applications in Heavy Industry

Recent applications of MAC analysis in heavy industry demonstrate its utility across sectors:

- **Steel industry**: Studies using MAC curves have identified electric arc furnace technology and green hydrogen reduction as cost-effective pathways, with average abatement costs of $120-300/tCO2 (Vázquez et al., 2023).
- **Cement industry**: MAC analyses reveal carbon capture and alternative fuels as dominant strategies, though costs remain high at $80-150/tCO2 (Shen et al., 2023).
- **Chemical industry**: Limited MAC studies exist for petrochemicals, though early work by Reiner et al. (2022) suggests electrification costs of $200-500/tCO2 for ethylene production.

### 2.2.2. Dynamic MAC Modeling Advancements

Traditional static MAC analyses have evolved into dynamic optimization models that incorporate learning curves, technology diffusion, and temporal constraints (Deng et al., 2023). These models better reflect real-world deployment dynamics, where technology costs decline with cumulative deployment and infrastructure constraints create path dependencies (Krey et al., 2023).

Recent methodological advances integrate MAC analysis with energy system models, enabling assessment of cross-sectoral interactions and indirect effects (Pietzcker et al., 2023). For the petrochemical sector, such integration is crucial given the interdependencies with electricity grids, hydrogen infrastructure, and feedstock markets.

## 2.3. Policy Instruments for Industrial Decarbonization

### 2.3.1. Carbon Contracts for Difference (CCfD)

Carbon Contracts for Difference have emerged as a promising policy mechanism to de-risk private investment in decarbonization technologies (Acosta et al., 2023). Recent European pilots demonstrate that CCfDs can effectively bridge the "valley of death" for first-mover projects, particularly in sectors with high capital intensity like chemicals (European Commission, 2023).

The UK's CCfD scheme for industrial decarbonization offers $21/tCO2 for 15-year contracts, projected to leverage $12 billion in private investment (UK Department for Business, Energy & Industrial Strategy, 2023). German models suggest CCfDs can reduce the levelized cost of hydrogen production by 30-40% through revenue certainty (Jägemann et al., 2023).

### 2.3.2. Industrial Strategy and Infrastructure Support

State-led infrastructure investment has proven critical for industrial cluster transitions. The Port of Rotterdam's hydrogen backbone project demonstrates how public infrastructure can enable private sector decarbonization at scale (Rotterdam Port Authority, 2023). Similarly, South Korea's "Hydrogen Economy Roadmap" includes $7.2 billion in public funding for hydrogen infrastructure through 2030 (Korean Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy, 2023).

### 2.3.3. Market-Based Mechanisms

Carbon pricing, border adjustments, and low-carbon product standards create market signals that drive industrial decarbonization (Böhringer et al., 2023). The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) is projected to increase costs for imported petrochemicals by 5-15%, creating competitive advantages for domestic green production (European Commission, 2023).

## 2.4. Research Gap and Contribution

While existing literature provides valuable insights into individual decarbonization technologies and policy mechanisms, several critical gaps remain:

1. **Sector-Specific Integration**: Few studies provide comprehensive, facility-level MAC analysis specifically for the petrochemical sector, which faces unique challenges due to feedstock emissions and complex process integration (Davis et al., 2023).

2. **Dynamic Assessment**: Limited research incorporates technology learning rates, infrastructure constraints, and temporal emission targets into petrochemical decarbonization pathways (Morris et al., 2022).

3. **Regional Specificity**: Most analyses focus on European or North American contexts, lacking detailed assessment of Asian industrial clusters where the majority of global petrochemical production occurs (IEA, 2024).

4. **Policy Integration**: Insufficient research links quantitative cost assessments with specific policy design recommendations for emerging economies like South Korea (Acosta et al., 2023).

This study addresses these gaps by developing a dynamic, facility-level MAC model for the South Korean petrochemical sector—representing one of the world's largest production hubs. Our integration of technology cost optimization with science-based carbon pathways provides novel insights into both the technical feasibility and policy requirements for achieving net-zero emissions in this critical industrial sector.
