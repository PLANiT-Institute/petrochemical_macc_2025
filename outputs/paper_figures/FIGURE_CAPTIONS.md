## Figure 1: Six-Scenario Cost Comparison

**File**: `Figure1_Six_Scenario_Comparison.png/pdf`

**Caption**:
> **Figure 1. Decarbonization cost comparison across six scenarios (2025-2050).** (a) Cumulative capital expenditure (CAPEX) for complete decarbonization by 2050 under three production pathways (Shaheen growth, 25% reduction, 40% reduction) and two technology routes (NCC-H₂ and NCC-Electricity). (b) Cost premium of NCC-Electricity pathway over NCC-H₂ pathway. NCC-H₂ maintains a 5.7-10.3% cost advantage despite literature-validated hydrogen consumption (0.56 ton H₂/ton ethylene). Production restructuring reduces total costs by 52-59% compared to growth pathway.

**Key Insights**:
- NCC-H₂ is consistently cheaper across all production pathways
- 40% reduction scenario achieves lowest cost ($13.0B for H₂, $14.5B for Electricity)
- Technology choice matters less than production pathway choice

## Figure 2: Technology Deployment Breakdown

**File**: `Figure2_Technology_Deployment.png/pdf`

**Caption**:
> **Figure 2. Technology deployment mix by scenario in 2050.** Pie charts show the relative contribution of each decarbonization technology to total abatement (MtCO₂). NCC technologies (either H₂-fueled or electrically-heated) dominate deployment (45-57%), followed by renewable electricity power purchase agreements (RE PPA, 12-38%) and industrial heat pumps (5-19%). Total deployment ranges from 23.7 Mt (40% reduction) to 43.0 Mt (Shaheen growth) of CO₂ abatement.

**Key Insights**:
- NCC technologies are the primary decarbonization lever
- RE PPA more important in growth scenarios
- Heat pumps contribute 5-19% depending on scenario

## Figure 3: MACC Curves Evolution

**File**: `Figure3_MACC_Curves.png/pdf`

**Caption**:
> **Figure 3. Marginal Abatement Cost Curves (MACC) for 2025, 2030, and 2050.** Each technology is represented as a horizontal bar with width proportional to abatement potential (MtCO₂) and height indicating abatement cost (USD/tCO₂). Technology costs decrease over time due to learning effects, with NCC technologies showing the steepest cost reductions (50-60% by 2050). Heat pumps and RE PPA have limited abatement potential (<10 MtCO₂) but remain cost-competitive throughout the period.

**Key Insights**:
- All technologies show cost reductions 2025→2050
- NCC technologies have highest abatement potential (>30 MtCO₂)
- Cost ordering remains stable: Heat Pump > NCC ≈ $120-240/tCO₂

## Figure 4: Technology Cost Evolution

**File**: `Figure4_Cost_Evolution.png/pdf`

**Caption**:
> **Figure 4. Technology cost trajectories and learning effects (2025-2050).** (a) Absolute abatement costs showing convergence between NCC-H₂ and NCC-Electricity pathways from initial 4% differential to 2% by 2050. (b) Cost reduction relative to 2025 baseline, demonstrating strong learning effects for NCC technologies (55-60% reduction) and moderate effects for heat pumps (40% reduction). RE PPA costs decline primarily due to projected renewable electricity price decreases.

**Key Insights**:
- Learning curves reduce NCC costs by 55-60% over 25 years
- NCC-H₂ and NCC-Electricity converge to similar costs by 2050
- Heat pump costs decline 40% due to technology maturation

## Figure 5: Hydrogen Demand Trajectories

**File**: `Figure5_Hydrogen_Demand.png/pdf`

**Caption**:
> **Figure 5. Hydrogen demand under NCC-H₂ scenarios (2025-2050).** (a) Annual hydrogen consumption reaches 4.0-7.7 Mt H₂/year by 2050, requiring 8-15 GW of electrolyzer capacity at 80% capacity factor. Demand ramps up primarily during 2030-2040 as NCC deployment accelerates. (b) Cumulative hydrogen consumption totals 83-142 Mt H₂ over the 26-year period. Growth pathway (Shaheen) requires 72% more hydrogen than the 40% reduction pathway.

**Key Insights**:
- Peak annual demand: 7.7 Mt H₂/year (Shaheen scenario)
- Requires ~15 GW electrolyzer capacity (Shaheen) by 2050
- Rapid deployment needed 2030-2040 (>2 GW/year)

## Figure 6: Emissions Trajectories

**File**: `Figure6_Emissions_Trajectories.png/pdf`

**Caption**:
> **Figure 6. Emissions trajectories with technology deployment (2025-2050).** (a) Actual emissions under decarbonization scenarios compared to business-as-usual (BAU) baselines. With 50-year facility retirement, BAU emissions decline naturally by 44-67% by 2050. Technology deployment further reduces emissions to 11.8-25.2 MtCO₂ (17-37% of 2025 baseline). (b) Annual abatement showing steady deployment during 2030-2040 period, reaching 13-26 MtCO₂/year by 2040. NCC-H₂ and NCC-Electricity pathways achieve identical emissions outcomes within each production scenario.

**Key Insights**:
- Technology choice (H₂ vs Electricity) doesn't affect emissions outcome
- Natural facility retirement provides 44-67% reduction even without new tech
- Residual emissions 11.8-25.2 Mt require additional technologies (CCS, biomass)

## Figure 7: Baseline Emissions Structure

**File**: `Figure7_Baseline_Structure.png/pdf`

**Caption**:
> **Figure 7. Baseline emissions structure in 2025.** (a) Top 10 products by emissions, with ethylene dominating at 27.0 MtCO₂/year (41% of total), followed by propylene (13.6 Mt, 21%) and benzene (7.5 Mt, 11%). Total baseline emissions: 66.2 MtCO₂/year from 248 facilities. (b) Emissions by fuel type, with naphtha combustion accounting for 57% (37.9 Mt), followed by natural gas/LNG (18%, 11.8 Mt) and fuel gas (15%, 10.0 Mt). Electricity-related emissions comprise 13% (8.3 Mt) at current grid emission factor.

**Key Insights**:
- Ethylene production is largest emissions source (41%)
- Naphtha combustion dominates fuel mix (57%)
- 11 ethylene facilities emit 27.0 Mt (baseline intensity: 2.26 tCO₂/ton)
