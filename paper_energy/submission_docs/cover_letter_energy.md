Dear Editor,

I submit the manuscript entitled "Infrastructure Bottlenecks in Petrochemical Decarbonization: Facility-Level Evidence from South Korea (2025–2050)" for consideration as an Original Research article in Energy.

This study addresses a critical gap in energy system planning: quantifying the electricity and hydrogen infrastructure demands of petrochemical decarbonization at the facility level. Using a bottom-up transition model covering 243 facilities in South Korea's petrochemical sector—one of the world's largest production clusters—we evaluate eight scenarios spanning electrification versus hydrogen pathways, energy price trajectories, and technology learning configurations.

Three findings are directly relevant to Energy's scope in energy planning and integrated energy systems:

1. **Energy infrastructure tradeoff:** Electricity-centered decarbonization pathways require approximately 146 TWh/yr of added electricity demand by 2050 (a 25% increase in national generation capacity), while hydrogen-centered pathways require 4.5 Mt/yr of hydrogen plus 32 TWh/yr of additional electricity.

2. **Energy cost dominance:** Energy costs constitute 93–98% of total transition expenditures across all scenarios, with energy price assumptions alone determining a ~500 trillion KRW (~400 BUSD) difference in cumulative costs—establishing energy pricing policy as the primary transition lever.

3. **Infrastructure as binding constraint:** Technology constraints limiting grid access increase 2050 transition costs by 17.0 BUSD/yr (68% premium), demonstrating that infrastructure availability, not technology cost, is the binding constraint.

These findings reframe industrial decarbonization from a technology selection problem to an energy system planning challenge, with implications for grid expansion, hydrogen supply chain development, and energy price policy design. The facility-level analysis is transferable to petrochemical clusters in East Asia, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia.

This manuscript is original, has not been published elsewhere, and is not under review at another journal. All data and verification artifacts are packaged for reproducibility.

I believe Energy is the ideal venue for this work given its focus on energy planning, integrated energy systems, and cross-sectoral energy transitions.
