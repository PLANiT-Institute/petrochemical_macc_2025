\begin{abstract}
South Korea's petrochemical sector emits 52.0 MtCO\textsubscript{2} per year across 248 facilities. We develop an open, energy-based marginal abatement cost curve (MACC) model that links facility-level fuel use to four transition options: industrial heat pumps, renewable-power purchase agreements (RE PPA), electric naphtha crackers, and hydrogen-fired naphtha crackers. The model combines annually resolved price trajectories, technology parameters, and facility applicability to generate MACC estimates for 2025--2050 without using discounted cash-flow assumptions. In 2030 the electrification pathway (heat pumps + RE PPA + electric crackers) yields 29.0 MtCO\textsubscript{2} of abatement at costs of \$215--\$334/tCO\textsubscript{2}, while a hydrogen pathway delivers 30.9 MtCO\textsubscript{2} at \$1,150/tCO\textsubscript{2}. Declining renewable and hydrogen prices reduce 2050 costs to \$111/tCO\textsubscript{2} (heat pumps) and \$174/tCO\textsubscript{2} (electric crackers), with green hydrogen reaching \$276/tCO\textsubscript{2}. System-wide deployment scenarios show that an aggressive programme leaves 5.0 MtCO\textsubscript{2} residual emissions by 2050, whereas conservative roll-outs stabilise at 20.0 MtCO\textsubscript{2}. The paper documents the modelling architecture, highlights data consistency checks, and discusses infrastructure prerequisites for achieving deep industrial decarbonisation.

\textbf{Keywords:} Marginal Abatement Cost Curve; Petrochemical; Electrification; Green Hydrogen; Korea; Energy System Modelling
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The global petrochemical industry emits approximately 1.3 GtCO\textsubscript{2} annually, contributing 3--4\% of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions \citep{IEA2020}. South Korea's petrochemical cluster is both energy intensive and export oriented, accounting for 52 MtCO\textsubscript{2}/year (around 7\% of national emissions). The government's 2050 Carbon Neutrality Strategy calls for deep reductions while safeguarding industrial competitiveness. Existing studies often apply levelised cost approaches that obscure the energy balance between fossil fuels, hydrogen, and electricity. We address this gap with an energy-explicit MACC framework that retains clear system boundaries and is reproducible from raw data to policy-ready indicators.

This work answers three questions. First, what is the baseline emissions profile and energy usage of Korean petrochemical facilities? Second, how do key decarbonisation options compare in terms of incremental abatement and cost when energy flows are tracked explicitly? Third, what long-term deployment scenarios are consistent with technology readiness, infrastructure constraints, and simple annualisation assumptions? Our contributions are: (i) an open data pipeline that bridges facility data, technology catalogues, and scenario analysis; (ii) a MACC formulation grounded in physical energy balances; and (iii) transparent, auto-updating tables that connect model outputs to the manuscript.

\section{Data and Model Architecture}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Facility Baseline and Energy Intensities}

The baseline module aggregates \texttt{outputs/module\_01/baseline\_2025\_detailed.csv}, which covers 248 facilities divided into five product groups (Olefins, Aromatics, Polymers, Intermediates, Other). For facility $i$ and fuel $f$, baseline combustion energy $E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f}$ is derived from annual production $A_i$ and technology-specific energy intensity $\theta_{i,f}$:
\begin{equation}
E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f} = A_i \cdot \theta_{i,f}.
\end{equation}
Multiplying by emission factor $\mathrm{EF}_{f,y}$ yields facility emissions in year $y$. The business-as-usual (BAU) trajectory applies demand growth multipliers and (optionally) retirement schedules using \texttt{data/demand\_growth\_trajectory.csv}.

\subsection{Technology Catalogue and Applicability}

\texttt{data/technology\_parameters.csv} summarises the four technology options $t$ with characteristics such as coefficient of performance (COP), hydrogen or electricity demand, capital expenditure (CAPEX), operating expenditure (OPEX), lifetime $L_t$, and technology readiness level (TRL). Applicability matrices in \texttt{data/facility\_technology\_applicability.csv} define the set $\mathcal{I}_t$ of facilities eligible for technology $t$.

\subsection{Price and Emission Trajectories}

Hydrogen and renewable electricity price trajectories (\texttt{data/h2\_price\_trajectory.csv}, \texttt{data/re\_price\_trajectory.csv}), grid emission factors (\texttt{data/grid\_emission\_trajectory.csv}), and fossil fuel prices (\texttt{data/fuel\_price\_trajectory.csv}) provide annually resolved inputs. These trajectories drive both abatement potential---by altering residual emissions---and incremental energy costs.

\section{Energy-Based MACC Formulation}
\label{sec:macc}

For each technology $t$, facility $i$, fuel $f$, and model year $y$, the post-adoption energy requirement $E^{\mathrm{new}}_{i,f,t,y}$ equals the baseline demand adjusted by the technology-specific substitution factor $\phi_{t,f,y}$ and the deployment fraction $s_{i,t,y}$:
\begin{equation}
E^{\mathrm{new}}_{i,f,t,y} = s_{i,t,y} \, \phi_{t,f,y} \, E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f} + (1-s_{i,t,y}) \, E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f}.
\end{equation}
The abatement achieved by technology $t$ in year $y$ aggregates facility-level changes across fuels:
\begin{equation}
\Delta \mathrm{CO}_{2,t,y} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_t} \sum_{f} \left(E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f} - E^{\mathrm{new}}_{i,f,t,y}\right) \mathrm{EF}_{f,y}.
\label{eq:abatement}
\end{equation}

Incremental energy expenditure is measured relative to the baseline:
\begin{equation}
\Delta C^{\mathrm{energy}}_{t,y} = \sum_{f} \left(E^{\mathrm{new}}_{t,f,y} - E^{\mathrm{base}}_{t,f}\right)^{+} p_{f,y},
\end{equation}
where $(\cdot)^{+}$ denotes the positive component and $p_{f,y}$ denotes the fuel or electricity price. This reflects the model assumption that fuel savings do not offset capital or operating costs; only new energy purchases are counted.

Simple annualisation translates investment cost into annual equivalents without discounting:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CAPEX}_{\mathrm{ann},t,y} = \frac{\mathrm{CAPEX}_{t,y}}{L_t}, \qquad
\mathrm{MACC}_{t,y} = \frac{\mathrm{CAPEX}_{\mathrm{ann},t,y} + \mathrm{OPEX}_{t,y} + \Delta C^{\mathrm{energy}}_{t,y}}{\Delta \mathrm{CO}_{2,t,y}}.
\label{eq:macc}
\end{equation}
Equation \eqref{eq:macc} yields technology- and year-specific marginal abatement costs in \$\,tCO\textsubscript{2}\textsuperscript{-1}. The MACC module evaluates \eqref{eq:abatement}--\eqref{eq:macc} for 2025--2050, capturing both learning-induced CAPEX reductions and price trajectories.

\section{Implementation and Scenario Setup}
\label{sec:implementation}

The modelling workflow comprises four Python modules:
\begin{description}
    \item[Module 1 (Baseline):] constructs facility-level energy and emission inventories and optional BAU trajectories.
    \item[Module 2 (MACC):] computes annual MACCs by technology using the formulation in \Cref{sec:macc}.
    \item[Module 3 (Optimisation):] deploys technologies under scenario-specific constraints (e.g., rollout caps, mutual exclusivity between NCC electrification and hydrogen).
    \item[Module 4 (Financial):] evaluates discounted cash flows for scenario outputs; this module is not used in the MACC values reported here.
\end{description}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Baseline Emissions}

\Cref{tab:product_group_energy_mix} reveals that Olefins (steam crackers producing ethylene/propylene) contribute 46.7 MtCO\textsubscript{2} (90\% of the baseline), fueled primarily by naphtha (71.5\%) and fuel gas (14.1\%). Aromatics plants (37 facilities) emit 5.1 MtCO\textsubscript{2} with a mixed LNG/fuel-gas supply. Polymers and Intermediates are electricity-dominated and together emit only 0.1 MtCO\textsubscript{2}.
\Cref{fig:baseline_product} visualises the baseline composition, underscoring the need to target steam crackers for deep abatement.

\subsection{Technology MACCs}

\Cref{tab:macc_snapshot} and the detailed 2030 MACC in \Cref{tab:macc_2030} show that heat pumps deliver the cheapest abatement (\$215/tCO\textsubscript{2}) but are limited to BTX/utility heat loads (0.87 MtCO\textsubscript{2}). RE PPA offers 7.68 MtCO\textsubscript{2} at \$319/tCO\textsubscript{2}. Electrifying steam crackers abates 20.47 MtCO\textsubscript{2} at \$334/tCO\textsubscript{2}, whereas the hydrogen cracker option abates 22.40 MtCO\textsubscript{2} at \$1,150/tCO\textsubscript{2}. Because hydrogen and electric crackers compete for the same facilities, their contributions are mutually exclusive.

\subsection{Scenario Outcomes}

\Cref{tab:scenario_summary} summarises three deployment archetypes. The Conservative pathway prioritises mature options and achieves a 61.5\% reduction by 2050. The Moderate pathway integrates electric crackers from 2030 with partial hydrogen uptake, reaching an 80.8\% reduction. The Aggressive pathway sustains rapid electrification and RE procurement, leaving 5.0 MtCO\textsubscript{2} residual emissions (90.4\% reduction) by 2050.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{System boundaries.} The energy-based formulation clarifies that naphtha remains a feedstock; only combustion energy is displaced. RE PPA affects solely the residual grid purchases after technology deployment, avoiding double counting with electrification.

\paragraph{Hydrogen versus electricity.} Although hydrogen offers slightly higher abatement potential, it is costlier than electrification throughout 2025--2050 under the assumed price trajectory. Hybrid operation (partial hydrogen blending) is a promising but as-yet unmodelled extension.

\paragraph{Infrastructure needs.} Achieving the Aggressive scenario requires roughly 60 TWh of additional renewable generation by 2050 and large-scale electrolyser deployment, implying significant grid and hydrogen infrastructure upgrades.

\paragraph{Unmodelled dynamics.} The MACC values rely on simple annualisation (no discounting). Financial decision-making typically applies discount rates of 5--8\%, so capital-intensive options will appear somewhat less attractive under discounted cash-flow assessments. Modules for Monte Carlo uncertainty, spatial siting constraints, and carbon capture integration remain areas for future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

An energy-explicit MACC framework for Korea's petrochemical industry shows that technology choice and sequencing matter. By 2030, electrification and renewable procurement can mitigate 29.0 MtCO\textsubscript{2} at \$215--\$334/tCO\textsubscript{2}; hydrogen pathways provide similar abatement but at higher cost. Continued declines in renewable energy and hydrogen prices shrink cost differentials by 2050, with aggressive deployment leaving a manageable 5.0 MtCO\textsubscript{2} residual. The modelling stack---from facility data to LaTeX-ready tables---is fully automated, enabling rapid re-analysis as new data arrive. Policymakers should combine technology deployment with grid reinforcement, hydrogen infrastructure planning, and market instruments that translate these cost curves into investment signals.

\section{Illustrative Cost Calculations}
\label{app:cost}

The heat pump example clarifies the simple annualisation convention. For a 2030 CAPEX of \$720 M/MtCO\textsubscript{2} and lifetime $L=20$ years,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CAPEX}_{\mathrm{ann}} = \frac{720}{20} = 36 \text{ \$/tCO\textsubscript{2}}.
\end{equation}
With OPEX = 3\% of CAPEX (21.6 \$/tCO\textsubscript{2}) and energy cost 157 \$/tCO\textsubscript{2}, the total MACC is \$215/tCO\textsubscript{2}, matching \Cref{tab:macc_2030}. Electric and hydrogen cracker costs follow the same logic using their respective electricity and hydrogen consumption.
