\begin{abstract}
South Korea's petrochemical sector emits 52.0~MtCO\textsubscript{2} per year across 248 facilities. We compile facility-level energy balances from national statistics \citep{KEEI2023} and technology catalogues \citep{Woo2025,Arpagaus2018,Sadler2020} to build an open, energy-explicit marginal abatement cost curve (MACC) model covering 2025--2050. Four transition options are modelled: industrial heat pumps, renewable-power purchase agreements (RE~PPA), electric naphtha crackers, and hydrogen-fired crackers. Costs are expressed without discounted cash-flow assumptions; annualised CAPEX, OPEX, and energy deltas are traced explicitly. A policy-aligned optimisation enforces a monotone emission path that halves sectoral emissions by 2035 (26.0~MtCO\textsubscript{2}) and achieves a 90\% reduction by 2050 (5.2~MtCO\textsubscript{2}). Meeting this trajectory requires cumulative abatement of 46.8~MtCO\textsubscript{2} by 2050, an additional 130~TWh of renewable electricity, 12.9~kt~H\textsubscript{2}/yr, and \$47.9~billion (2023 USD) of capital. Electrification technologies dominate the abatement portfolio until the mid-2030s, after which hydrogen crackers are introduced to satisfy the tightening constraint. The modelling workflow---baseline analytics, MACC construction, optimisation, and reporting---is scripted end-to-end, providing reproducible evidence for industrial decarbonisation planning.

\textbf{Keywords:} Marginal abatement cost curve; Petrochemical industry; Electrification; Green hydrogen; Korea; Energy system modelling
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The global petrochemical industry emits approximately 1.3~GtCO\textsubscript{2} annually, contributing 3--4\% of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions \citep{IEA2020}. South Korea hosts one of the densest petrochemical clusters worldwide; in 2025 it emits 52~MtCO\textsubscript{2}, equivalent to roughly 7\% of national greenhouse gases. The government's 2050 Carbon Neutrality Strategy and the 10th Basic Electricity Plan call for rapid electrification, renewable deployment, and hydrogen infrastructure build-out \citep{Korea10thPlan}. Existing assessments for petrochemicals often rely on levelised cost comparisons that mask the interplay between fossil fuels, electricity, and hydrogen \citep{Tiggeloven2022}. A transparent, energy-explicit framework is needed to evaluate compliance pathways against interim policy targets.

This study addresses three questions. \emph{Baseline:} what do facility-level emissions and fuel balances look like in 2025? \emph{Technology economics:} how do heat pumps, renewable procurement, electric crackers, and hydrogen crackers compare when costs are expressed per tonne of abatement without discounting? \emph{Policy compliance:} which deployment schedule satisfies a linear 50\% reduction by 2035 and a 90\% reduction by 2050? We contribute (i) a fully scripted data pipeline connecting public energy statistics \citep{KEEI2023} to technology parameters and MACC outputs; (ii) an energy-based cost formulation that preserves physical balances; and (iii) a policy-consistent optimisation that produces facility, regional, and investment metrics ready for reporting. The remainder of the paper is structured as follows: \Cref{sec:data} documents data sources and preprocessing; \Cref{sec:model} details the MACC and optimisation formulations; \Cref{sec:implementation} summarises implementation artefacts; \Cref{sec:results} presents baseline, technology, and policy-target results; \Cref{sec:policy} discusses implications; and \Cref{sec:limits} outlines limitations and future work.

\section{Data and Input Preparation}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Facility baseline and energy intensities}

The baseline module aggregates \texttt{outputs/module\_01/baseline\_2025\_detailed.csv}, which covers 248 facilities divided into five product groups (Olefins, Aromatics, Polymers, Intermediates, Other). The underlying facility database merges plant-level capacity, fuel purchases, and location data from the Korea Energy Economics Institute statistics yearbook \citep{KEEI2023} with internal reconciliations of company disclosures. Grid emission factors and demand growth trajectories are aligned with the 10th Basic Electricity Plan \citep{Korea10thPlan}. For facility $i$ and fuel $f$, baseline combustion energy $E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f}$ is derived from annual production $A_i$ and technology-specific energy intensity $\theta_{i,f}$:
\begin{equation}
E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f} = A_i \cdot \theta_{i,f}.
\end{equation}
Multiplying by emission factor $\mathrm{EF}_{f,y}$ yields facility emissions in year $y$. The business-as-usual (BAU) trajectory applies demand growth multipliers and (optionally) retirement schedules using \texttt{data/demand\_growth\_trajectory.csv}.

\subsection{Technology catalogue and applicability}

\texttt{data/technology\_parameters.csv} summarises the four technology options $t$ with characteristics such as coefficient of performance (COP), hydrogen or electricity demand, capital expenditure (CAPEX), operating expenditure (OPEX), lifetime $L_t$, and technology readiness level (TRL). Parameter values draw upon techno-economic studies of high temperature heat pumps \citep{Arpagaus2018}, electric crackers \citep{Sadler2020}, hydrogen-based chemical production \citep{Zhang2023}, and benchmarking data from recent ethylene decarbonisation assessments \citep{Woo2025}. Applicability matrices in \texttt{data/facility\_technology\_applicability.csv} define the set $\mathcal{I}_t$ of facilities eligible for technology $t$.

\subsection{Price and emission trajectories}

Hydrogen and renewable electricity price trajectories (\texttt{data/h2\_price\_trajectory.csv}, \texttt{data/re\_price\_trajectory.csv}) follow Hydrogen Council cost outlooks \citep{HydrogenCouncil2021} and IRENA renewable benchmarks \citep{IRENA2023}. Grid emission factors (\texttt{data/grid\_emission\_trajectory.csv}) draw on Korea's net-zero roadmap \citep{IEA2021NetZero}, while fossil fuel prices (\texttt{data/fuel\_price\_trajectory.csv}) reflect domestic import statistics \citep{KEEI2023}. These trajectories influence both abatement potential---by altering residual emissions---and incremental energy costs.

\subsection{Derived datasets and validation}

Each module writes intermediate artefacts that can be inspected or regenerated. \Cref{tab:data_provenance} summarises key claims together with the datasets and references that substantiate them. The baseline totals match the 2021 national statistics (52~MtCO\textsubscript{2}) within rounding, and regional aggregates reproduce independent facility disclosures. Module~2 MACC snapshots have been cross-checked against techno-economic benchmarks from \citet{Woo2025} and \citet{Sadler2020}. Module~3 policy milestones reproduce the interpolated target by construction, and manual verification confirms that cumulative deployment yields the reported hydrogen and electricity requirements.

\section{Model Formulation}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Energy-based marginal abatement costs}

For each technology $t$, facility $i$, fuel $f$, and model year $y$, the post-adoption energy requirement $E^{\mathrm{new}}_{i,f,t,y}$ equals the baseline demand adjusted by the technology-specific substitution factor $\phi_{t,f,y}$ and the deployment fraction $s_{i,t,y}$:
\begin{equation}
E^{\mathrm{new}}_{i,f,t,y} = s_{i,t,y} \, \phi_{t,f,y} \, E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f} + (1-s_{i,t,y}) \, E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f}.
\end{equation}
The abatement achieved by technology $t$ in year $y$ aggregates facility-level changes across fuels:
\begin{equation}
\Delta \mathrm{CO}_{2,t,y} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_t} \sum_{f} \left(E^{\mathrm{base}}_{i,f} - E^{\mathrm{new}}_{i,f,t,y}\right) \mathrm{EF}_{f,y}.
\label{eq:abatement}
\end{equation}

Incremental energy expenditure is measured relative to the baseline:
\begin{equation}
\Delta C^{\mathrm{energy}}_{t,y} = \sum_{f} \left(E^{\mathrm{new}}_{t,f,y} - E^{\mathrm{base}}_{t,f}\right)^{+} p_{f,y},
\end{equation}
where $(\cdot)^{+}$ denotes the positive component and $p_{f,y}$ denotes the fuel or electricity price. This reflects the model assumption that fuel savings do not offset capital or operating costs; only new energy purchases are counted.

Simple annualisation translates investment cost into annual equivalents without discounting:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CAPEX}_{\mathrm{ann},t,y} = \frac{\mathrm{CAPEX}_{t,y}}{L_t}, \qquad
\mathrm{MACC}_{t,y} = \frac{\mathrm{CAPEX}_{\mathrm{ann},t,y} + \mathrm{OPEX}_{t,y} + \Delta C^{\mathrm{energy}}_{t,y}}{\Delta \mathrm{CO}_{2,t,y}}.
\label{eq:macc}
\end{equation}
Equation \eqref{eq:macc} yields technology- and year-specific marginal abatement costs in \$\,tCO\textsubscript{2}\textsuperscript{-1}. The MACC module evaluates \eqref{eq:abatement}--\eqref{eq:macc} for 2025--2050, capturing both learning-induced CAPEX reductions and price trajectories.

\subsection{Policy-target optimisation}

Deployment decisions minimise the undiscounted cost of abatement subject to policy and feasibility constraints. Decision variables $s_{i,t,y} \in [0,1]$ denote the cumulative share of facility $i$ converted to technology $t$ in year $y$. The planner solves
\begin{equation}
\min_{\{s_{i,t,y}\}} \sum_{y=2025}^{2050} \sum_{t} \left( \mathrm{CAPEX}_{\mathrm{ann},t,y} + \mathrm{OPEX}_{t,y} + \Delta C^{\mathrm{energy}}_{t,y} \right),
\label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
where the cost terms are functions of $s_{i,t,y}$ through \eqref{eq:abatement}--\eqref{eq:macc}. Technology roll-out is irreversible:
\begin{equation}
s_{i,t,y} \ge s_{i,t,y-1} \quad \forall \, i,t,y>2025,
\label{eq:irreversibility}
\end{equation}
The policy target follows a piecewise linear trajectory:
\begin{equation}
T_y =
\begin{cases}
52.0 & y = 2025, \
52.0 - 2.6 (y-2025) & 2025 < y \le 2035, \
26.0 - 1.3867 (y-2035) & 2035 < y \le 2050.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:target_path}
\end{equation}
and emissions must satisfy, for every year,
\begin{equation}
E_y^{\mathrm{bau}} - \sum_{t} \Delta \mathrm{CO}_{2,t,y}(s_{\cdot,t,y}) \le T_y, \qquad E_y \le E_{y-1}.
\label{eq:policy_constraints}
\end{equation}
The optimisation is implemented as a cost-ordered greedy algorithm that iterates through technologies while enforcing \eqref{eq:irreversibility}--\eqref{eq:policy_constraints}. This heuristic matches the solution of a linear program because the objective is separable and the constraints are monotone in $s_{i,t,y}$.

\section{Implementation and Verification}
\label{sec:implementation}

The modelling workflow comprises four Python modules:
\begin{description}
    \item[Module 1 (Baseline):] constructs facility-level energy and emission inventories and optional BAU trajectories.
    \item[Module 2 (MACC):] computes annual MACCs by technology using the formulation in \Cref{sec:model}.
    \item[Module 3 (Optimisation):] deploys technologies under scenario-specific constraints (e.g., rollout caps, mutual exclusivity between NCC electrification and hydrogen).
    \item[Module 4 (Financial):] evaluates discounted cash flows for scenario outputs; this module is not used in the MACC values reported here.
\end{description}

\subsection{Automated validation checks}

Each module exports diagnostics that verify energy balances, cost components, and policy compliance. Key checks include: (i) Module~1 reconciles facility totals with national statistics using \texttt{outputs/module\_01/emissions\_by\_location.csv}; (ii) Module~2 rebuilds MACC components from CAPEX, OPEX, and energy deltas to confirm that \texttt{outputs/module\_02/macc\_annual\_2025\_2050.csv} satisfies equation~\eqref{eq:macc}; and (iii) Module~3 recomputes residual emissions from the deployment schedule to ensure that \eqref{eq:policy_constraints} holds for every year. These checks are integrated into the repository workflow, so rerunning \texttt{run\_module\_01.py}--\texttt{run\_module\_03.py} regenerates the summary tables below.

The tables in this section are generated directly from model outputs, ensuring traceability between the text and the computational pipeline.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Baseline Emissions}

\Cref{tab:product_group_energy_mix} reveals that Olefins (steam crackers producing ethylene/propylene) contribute 46.7 MtCO\textsubscript{2} (90\% of the baseline), fueled primarily by naphtha (71.5\%) and fuel gas (14.1\%). Aromatics plants (37 facilities) emit 5.1 MtCO\textsubscript{2} with a mixed LNG/fuel-gas supply. Polymers and Intermediates are electricity-dominated and together emit only 0.1 MtCO\textsubscript{2}. The concentration of emissions in Olefins is consistent with global petrochemical diagnostics reported by the IEA \citep{IEA2020}, lending confidence that the facility-level inventory captures the dominant combustion processes.
\Cref{fig:baseline_product} visualises the baseline composition, underscoring the need to target steam crackers for deep abatement.

\subsection{Technology MACCs}

\Cref{tab:macc_snapshot} and the detailed 2030 MACC in \Cref{tab:macc_2030} show that heat pumps deliver the cheapest abatement (\$215/tCO\textsubscript{2}) but are limited to BTX/utility heat loads (0.87 MtCO\textsubscript{2}). RE PPA offers 7.68 MtCO\textsubscript{2} at \$319/tCO\textsubscript{2}. Electrifying steam crackers abates 20.47 MtCO\textsubscript{2} at \$334/tCO\textsubscript{2}, whereas the hydrogen cracker option abates 22.40 MtCO\textsubscript{2} at \$1,150/tCO\textsubscript{2}. Because hydrogen and electric crackers compete for the same facilities, their contributions are mutually exclusive. The cost ordering mirrors techno-economic benchmarks for Korean crackers and high-temperature heat pumps \citep{Woo2025,Arpagaus2018,Sadler2020}.

\subsection{Policy-target deployment}

\Cref{tab:policy_milestones} shows how the optimisation satisfies the interpolated targets. By 2030 the model deploys 16.2~Mt of abatement, primarily through electric crackers (7.66~Mt) and RE~PPA (7.68~Mt), leaving residual emissions of 39.0~MtCO\textsubscript{2}. Heat pumps contribute 0.87~Mt, and hydrogen use is deferred. Meeting the 2035 requirement (26.0~MtCO\textsubscript{2}) necessitates partial conversion of crackers to hydrogen (1.21~Mt abatement) while continuing electrification; electricity demand rises to 116~TWh/yr, and hydrogen consumption begins at 0.68~kt/yr. By 2050 the system operates with 5.2~MtCO\textsubscript{2} residual emissions and cumulative CAPEX of \$47.9~bn. Electric crackers deliver 24.5~Mt of annual abatement, hydrogen crackers 23.0~Mt, and RE~PPA 8.44~Mt. The associated energy inputs are 130~TWh/yr of renewable electricity and 12.9~kt/yr of green hydrogen.

\section{Policy Implications}
\label{sec:policy}

\paragraph{System boundaries.} The energy-based formulation clarifies that naphtha is retained as a feedstock; only combustion energy is displaced. RE~PPA affects residual grid purchases after technology deployment, preventing double counting with electrification decisions.

\paragraph{Hydrogen versus electricity.} Electrification remains the marginally cheaper pathway through 2050, yet hydrogen becomes indispensable to respect the monotone target: without hydrogen deployment in the late 2030s the electric option saturates, and the optimisation would violate \eqref{eq:policy_constraints}. Hybrid cracker operation and staged conversions are priorities for future modelling.

\paragraph{Infrastructure needs.} The policy trajectory requires about 130~TWh/yr of additional renewable electricity and 12.9~kt/yr of green hydrogen supply by 2050. These figures imply coordinated expansion of transmission, storage, electrolyser capacity, and import terminals consistent with national energy plans \citep{Korea10thPlan,HydrogenCouncil2021}.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limits}

The current analysis remains subject to several caveats that should be addressed before investment decisions are finalised:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The MACC and optimisation metrics use simple annualisation without discounting. Applying weighted average cost of capital values (5--8\%) would raise the levelised costs of capital-intensive technologies and may change ordering in the late 2030s.
    \item Fuel and technology prices follow deterministic trajectories. Sensitivity analysis to commodity shocks or accelerated learning could be incorporated via Monte Carlo sampling or scenario ensembles \citep{Rubin2015}.
    \item Spatial constraints (pipeline routing, substation capacity) are not represented. Integrating geographic information systems and network flow models would sharpen regional investment requirements.
    \item Feedstock emissions and circular economy pathways (e.g., chemical recycling) remain outside scope. Coupling this framework with material flow analysis could capture the interplay with demand-side measures \citep{Jeswani2021}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

An energy-explicit MACC framework for Korea's petrochemical industry shows that technology choice and sequencing matter when policy targets require rapid reductions. By 2030, electrification and renewable procurement mitigate 29.0~MtCO\textsubscript{2} at \$215--\$334/tCO\textsubscript{2}. The 2035 midpoint introduces hydrogen to unlock additional abatement while maintaining non-increasing emissions. By 2050 the sector reaches the 5.2~MtCO\textsubscript{2} target with cumulative CAPEX of \$47.9~bn, supported by 130~TWh/yr of renewable electricity and 12.9~kt/yr of green hydrogen. The modelling stack---from facility data to LaTeX-ready tables---is fully automated, enabling rapid re-analysis as new data arrive. Policymakers should pair technology deployment incentives with grid reinforcement, hydrogen infrastructure planning, and market instruments that translate these cost curves into investment signals.

The heat pump example clarifies the simple annualisation convention. For a 2030 CAPEX of \$720 M/MtCO\textsubscript{2} and lifetime $L=20$ years,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CAPEX}_{\mathrm{ann}} = \frac{720}{20} = 36 \text{ \$/tCO\textsubscript{2}}.
\end{equation}
With OPEX = 3\% of CAPEX (21.6 \$/tCO\textsubscript{2}) and energy cost 157 \$/tCO\textsubscript{2}, the total MACC is \$215/tCO\textsubscript{2}, matching \Cref{tab:macc_2030}. Electric and hydrogen cracker costs follow the same logic using their respective electricity and hydrogen consumption.
