## Manuscript Summary

Industrial decarbonization pathways are typically assessed using Marginal Abatement Cost Curve (MACC) methodology, which evaluates technologies based on cost per ton of CO₂ abated while treating energy supply as unconstrained. This partial equilibrium approach systematically excludes critical energy system integration constraints. Through facility-level modeling of South Korea's petrochemical sector (248 facilities, 11.96 million tons ethylene capacity annually), we demonstrate that hydrogen and electricity decarbonization pathways show nearly identical costs ($31.4 billion vs $33.3 billion, 2025-2050)—suggesting both are economically viable. However, energy system integration analysis reveals that the electricity pathway requires 164.5 TWh of additional annual demand by 2050, equivalent to 29.5% of Korea's current total electricity consumption and 96.8% of the government's 2036 renewable electricity target. This demand from petrochemicals alone—excluding competing needs from transport, buildings, and other industries—renders the pathway physically infeasible. In contrast, the hydrogen pathway requires 7.7 million tons H₂ annually (27.6% of Korea's planned supply), which can be met through dedicated off-grid infrastructure and imports, avoiding grid competition.

## Significance for Carbon Neutrality Readership

This manuscript addresses a critical gap in industrial decarbonization literature and provides three principal contributions relevant to *Carbon Neutrality*'s scope:

**1. Methodological Innovation**: We demonstrate how standard MACC models systematically underestimate electricity pathway barriers by excluding energy system constraints. Our enhanced framework—integrating facility-level MACC with explicit energy system demand quantification—reveals binding constraints invisible to conventional partial equilibrium analysis. This methodology is applicable to industrial decarbonization assessment globally, not only for petrochemicals but for steel, cement, and other energy-intensive sectors.

**2. Policy-Relevant Empirical Findings**: For South Korea specifically, our results validate the Hydrogen Economy Roadmap (2019, updated 2021) as necessary infrastructure investment rather than speculative commitment, while demonstrating that the 10th Basic Plan for Electricity Supply and Demand's renewable targets are insufficient to support industrial electrification alongside competing sectoral demands. These findings have immediate relevance as Korea finalizes its NDC enhancement and long-term industrial strategy.

**3. Global Generalizability**: While focused on South Korea, our findings apply to other industrialized countries facing similar resource constraints—EU, Japan, and OECD economies. The binding constraint that aggregate industrial electricity demands can exceed national renewable targets applies wherever grid capacity growth faces physical, economic, or political limits. Countries currently deciding between electrification-first versus hydrogen infrastructure strategies will find this framework directly applicable.

## Timeliness and Impact

The paper addresses urgent policy questions as governments worldwide finalize net-zero implementation plans for 2030-2050. The "electrify everything" paradigm dominates current discourse, yet our analysis reveals that grid capacity constraints—not technology costs—may prove the binding constraint for industrial decarbonization. This insight transforms policy priorities from technology-neutral incentives (assuming unlimited capacity) to strategic infrastructure planning that aligns sectoral pathways with system-level constraints.
