I am pleased to submit our manuscript entitled "Beyond Partial Equilibrium: Why Technology Costs and Energy System Constraints Diverge in Industrial Decarbonization Pathways" for consideration for publication in Carbon Neutrality.

This research addresses a critical gap in industrial decarbonization analysis: the disconnect between technology cost assessments and energy system feasibility. Through facility-level modeling of South Korea's petrochemical sector (248 facilities, 66.2 MtCO₂/year), we demonstrate that hydrogen and electricity pathways show near-identical costs ($31.4B vs $33.3B, 6% difference) yet diverge by orders of magnitude in system integration requirements. The electricity pathway requires 164.5 TWh annually—consuming 97% of Korea's 2036 renewable target for one sector alone—while the hydrogen pathway requires 7.7 Mt H₂ (28% of 2050 target).

This work makes three key contributions aligned with Carbon Neutrality's mission:

1. **Methodological**: We extend MACC analysis beyond partial equilibrium to explicitly quantify aggregate energy demands, revealing that grid capacity—not technology cost—emerges as the binding constraint for industrial decarbonization.

2. **Empirical**: Facility-level resolution (11 naphtha cracking complexes) enables precise energy demand quantification, contrasting with sector-aggregated studies that systematically underestimate infrastructure constraints.

3. **Policy**: Our findings validate hydrogen infrastructure as necessary rather than optional for heavy industry, with implications extending to EU, Japan, and resource-constrained OECD economies. This directly supports carbon neutrality pathway planning by demonstrating that achieving net-zero requires configuring national energy systems to accommodate sectoral demands within infrastructure constraints.

The manuscript presents original research not under consideration elsewhere. All data and code are publicly available on GitHub (https://github.com/justjinsu/korea_petrochemical) to ensure reproducibility. The research was conducted independently with no funding sources or competing interests.

We believe this work will be of significant interest to Carbon Neutrality's readership, particularly researchers and policymakers engaged in industrial decarbonization strategy, energy system planning, and carbon neutrality pathway development.

Thank you for considering our manuscript. I look forward to your response.
