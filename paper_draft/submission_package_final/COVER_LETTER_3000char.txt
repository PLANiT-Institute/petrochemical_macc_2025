Dear Editor-in-Chief,

I am pleased to submit "Beyond Partial Equilibrium: Why Technology Costs and Energy System Constraints Diverge in Industrial Decarbonization Pathways" for publication in Carbon Neutrality.

This research addresses a critical gap in industrial decarbonization: the disconnect between technology-level cost assessments and energy system feasibility. Through facility-level modeling of South Korea's petrochemical sector (248 facilities, 66.2 MtCO₂/year, 13% of national emissions), we demonstrate that hydrogen and electricity pathways show near-identical costs ($31.4B vs $33.3B, 6% difference) yet diverge dramatically in system integration. The electricity pathway demands 164.5 TWh annually—consuming 97% of Korea's 2036 renewable target for one sector alone—while hydrogen requires 7.7 Mt H₂ (28% of 2050 target).

Our findings make three contributions aligned with Carbon Neutrality's mission:

First, methodologically, we extend Marginal Abatement Cost Curve (MACC) analysis beyond partial equilibrium to explicitly quantify aggregate energy demands. This reveals that grid capacity—not technology cost—emerges as the binding constraint, a finding systematically obscured by conventional MACC approaches evaluating technologies in isolation.

Second, empirically, facility-level resolution (11 naphtha cracking complexes) enables precise energy demand quantification validated against Korea's energy policy targets. Sensitivity analysis confirms findings persist across ±10% parameter variations.

Third, for policy, our results validate hydrogen infrastructure as necessary rather than optional for heavy industry, with implications extending to EU, Japan, and resource-constrained OECD economies. The divergence between technology costs and system feasibility documented here directly informs carbon neutrality pathway planning by demonstrating that achieving net-zero requires configuring national energy systems to accommodate sectoral demands within infrastructure constraints.

The manuscript presents original research not under consideration elsewhere. All model code, parameters, and results are publicly available on GitHub (https://github.com/justjinsu/korea_petrochemical) to ensure reproducibility. The research was conducted independently with no funding or competing interests.

We believe this work addresses Carbon Neutrality's core mission by providing quantitative evidence for technology-system co-optimization in industrial climate policy.

Thank you for considering our manuscript.
